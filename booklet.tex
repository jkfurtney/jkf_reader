% ── Poem 1 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
\poemtitle{Ulysses}
\poemauthor{Alfred Lord Tennyson}

\begin{verse}
It little profits that an idle king,\\
By this still hearth, among these barren crags,\\
Match'd with an aged wife, I mete and dole\\
Unequal laws unto a savage race,\\
That hoard, and sleep, and feed, and know not me.\\
I cannot rest from travel: I will drink\\
Life to the lees: All times I have enjoy'd\\
Greatly, have suffer'd greatly, both with those\\
That loved me, and alone, on shore, and when\\
Thro' scudding drifts the rainy Hyades\\
Vext the dim sea: I am become a name;\\
For always roaming with a hungry heart\\
Much have I seen and known; cities of men\\
And manners, climates, councils, governments,\\
Myself not least, but honour'd of them all;\\
And drunk delight of battle with my peers,\\
Far on the ringing plains of windy Troy.\\
I am a part of all that I have met;\\
Yet all experience is an arch wherethro'\\
Gleams that untravell'd world whose margin fades\\
For ever and forever when I move.\\
How dull it is to pause, to make an end,\\
To rust unburnish'd, not to shine in use!\\
As tho' to breathe were life! Life piled on life\\
Were all too little, and of one to me\\
Little remains: but every hour is saved\\
From that eternal silence, something more,\\
A bringer of new things; and vile it were\\
For some three suns to store and hoard myself,\\
And this gray spirit yearning in desire\\
To follow knowledge like a sinking star,\\
Beyond the utmost bound of human thought.\\!

\vin         This is my son, mine own Telemachus,\\
To whom I leave the sceptre and the isle,—\\
Well-loved of me, discerning to fulfil\\
This labour, by slow prudence to make mild\\
A rugged people, and thro' soft degrees\\
Subdue them to the useful and the good.\\
Most blameless is he, centred in the sphere\\
Of common duties, decent not to fail\\
In offices of tenderness, and pay\\
Meet adoration to my household gods,\\
When I am gone. He works his work, I mine.\\!

\vin         There lies the port; the vessel puffs her sail:\\
There gloom the dark, broad seas. My mariners,\\
Souls that have toil'd, and wrought, and thought with me—\\
That ever with a frolic welcome took\\
The thunder and the sunshine, and opposed\\
Free hearts, free foreheads—you and I are old;\\
Old age hath yet his honour and his toil;\\
Death closes all: but something ere the end,\\
Some work of noble note, may yet be done,\\
Not unbecoming men that strove with Gods.\\
The lights begin to twinkle from the rocks:\\
The long day wanes: the slow moon climbs: the deep\\
Moans round with many voices. Come, my friends,\\
'T is not too late to seek a newer world.\\
Push off, and sitting well in order smite\\
The sounding furrows; for my purpose holds\\
To sail beyond the sunset, and the baths\\
Of all the western stars, until I die.\\
It may be that the gulfs will wash us down:\\
It may be we shall touch the Happy Isles,\\
And see the great Achilles, whom we knew.\\
Tho' much is taken, much abides; and tho'\\
We are not now that strength which in old days\\
Moved earth and heaven, that which we are, we are;\\
One equal temper of heroic hearts,\\
Made weak by time and fate, but strong in will\\
To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield.\\!

\end{verse}

% ── Poem 2 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
\poemtitle{Childe Roland to the Dark Tower Came}
\poemauthor{Robert Browning}
{\small\itshape (See Edgar's song in ``Lear.'')\par}\medskip

\begin{verse}
1.\\
My first thought was, he lied in every word,\\
\vin That hoary cripple, with malicious eye\\
\vin Askance to watch the working of his lie\\
On mine, and mouth scarce able to afford\\
Suppression of the glee that pursed and scored\\
\vin Its edge at one more victim gained thereby.\\!

2.\\
What else should he be set for, with his staff?\\
\vin What, save to waylay with his lies, ensnare\\
\vin All travellers who might find him posted there,\\
And ask the road? I guessed what skull-like laugh\\
Would break, what crutch 'gin write my epitaph\\
\vin For pastime in the dusty thoroughfare,\\!

3.\\
If at his counsel I should turn aside\\
\vin Into that ominous tract which, all agree,\\
\vin Hides the Dark Tower. Yet acquiescingly\\
I did turn as he pointed; neither pride\\
Nor hope rekindling at the end descried,\\
\vin So much as gladness that some end might be.\\!

4.\\
For, what with my whole world-wide wandering,\\
\vin What with my search drawn out thro' years, my hope\\
\vin Dwindled into a ghost not fit to cope\\
With that obstreperous joy success would bring,---\\
I hardly tried now to rebuke the spring\\
\vin My heart made, finding failure in its scope.\\!

5.\\
As when a sick man very near to death\\
\vin Seems dead indeed, and feels begin and end\\
\vin The tears and takes the farewell of each friend,\\
And hears one bid the other go, draw breath\\
Freelier outside, (``since all is o'er'' he saith,\\
\vin ``And the blow fall'n no grieving can amend'')\\!

6.\\
While some discuss if near the other graves\\
\vin Be room enough for this, and when a day\\
\vin Suits best for carrying the corpse away,\\
With care about the banners, scarves and staves,---\\
And still the man hears all, and only craves\\
\vin He may not shame such tender love and stay.\\!

7.\\
Thus, I had so long suffered in this quest,\\
\vin Heard failure prophesied so oft, been writ\\
\vin So many times among ``The Band''---to wit,\\
The knights who to the Dark Tower's search addressed\\
Their steps---that just to fail as they, seemed best,\\
\vin And all the doubt was now---should I be fit.\\!

8.\\
So, quiet as despair, I turned from him,\\
\vin That hateful cripple, out of his highway\\
\vin Into the path he pointed. All the day\\
Had been a dreary one at best, and dim\\
Was settling to its close, yet shot one grim\\
\vin Red leer to see the plain catch its estray.\\!

9.\\
For mark! no sooner was I fairly found\\
\vin Pledged to the plain, after a pace or two,\\
\vin Than pausing to throw backward a last view\\
To the safe road, 'twas gone! gray plain all round!\\
Nothing but plain to the horizon's bound.\\
\vin I might go on; nought else remained to do.\\!

10.\\
So, on I went. I think I never saw\\
\vin Such starved ignoble nature; nothing throve:\\
\vin For flowers---as well expect a cedar grove!\\
But cockle, spurge, according to their law\\
Might propagate their kind, with none to awe,\\
\vin You'd think: a burr had been a treasure-trove.\\!

11.\\
No! penury, inertness, and grimace,\\
\vin In some strange sort, were the land's portion. ``See\\
\vin Or shut your eyes,'' ---said Nature peevishly---\\
``It nothing skills: I cannot help my case:\\
The Judgment's fire must cure this place,\\
\vin Calcine its clods and set my prisoners free.''\\!

12.\\
If there pushed any ragged thistle-stalk\\
\vin Above its mates, the head was chopped---the bents\\
\vin Were jealous else. What made those holes and rents\\
In the dock's harsh swarth leaves---bruised as to baulk\\
All hope of greenness? 'tis a brute must walk\\
\vin Pashing their life out, with a brute's intents.\\!

13.\\
As for the grass, it grew as scant as hair\\
\vin In leprosy---thin dry blades pricked the mud\\
\vin Which underneath looked kneaded up with blood.\\
One stiff blind horse, his every bone a-stare,\\
Stood stupefied, however he came there---\\
\vin Thrust out past service from the devil's stud!\\!

14.\\
Alive? he might be dead for all I know,\\
\vin With that red gaunt and colloped neck a-strain,\\
\vin And shut eyes underneath the rusty mane.\\
Seldom went such grotesqueness with such woe:\\
I never saw a brute I hated so---\\
\vin He must be wicked to deserve such pain.\\!

15.\\
I shut my eyes and turned them on my heart.\\
\vin As a man calls for wine before he fights,\\
\vin I asked one draught of earlier, happier sights,\\
Ere fitly I could hope to play my part.\\
Think first, fight afterwards---the soldier's art:\\
\vin One taste of the old time sets all to rights!\\!

16.\\
Not it! I fancied Cuthbert's reddening face\\
\vin Beneath its garniture of curly gold,\\
\vin Dear fellow, till I almost felt him fold\\
An arm in mine to fix me to the place,\\
That way he used. Alas! one night's disgrace!\\
\vin Out went my heart's new fire and left it cold.\\!

17.\\
Giles, then, the soul of honour---there he stands\\
\vin Frank as ten years ago when knighted first.\\
\vin What honest men should dare (he said) he durst.\\
Good---but the scene shifts---faugh! what hangman's\\
Pin to his breast a parchment? His own hands\\
\vin Read it. Poor traitor, spit upon and curst!\\!

18.\\
Better this present than a past like that---\\
\vin Back therefore to my darkening path again.\\
\vin No sound, no sight as far as eye could strain.\\
Will the night send a howlet or a bat?\\
I asked: when something on the dismal flat\\
\vin Came to arrest my thoughts and change their train.\\!

19.\\
A sudden little river crossed my path\\
\vin As unexpected as a serpent comes.\\
\vin No sluggish tide congenial to the glooms---\\
This, as it frothed by, might have been a bath\\
For the fiend's glowing hoof---to see the wrath\\
\vin Of its black eddy bespate with flakes and spumes.\\!

20.\\
So, petty yet so spiteful! all along\\
\vin Low scrubby alders kneeled down over it;\\
\vin Drenched willows flung them headlong in a fit\\
Of mute despair, a suicidal throng:\\
The river which had done them all the wrong,\\
\vin Whate'er that was, rolled by, deterred no whit.\\!

21.\\
Which, while I forded,---good saints, how I feared\\
\vin To set my foot upon a dead man's cheek,\\
\vin Each step, or feel the spear I thrust to seek\\
For hollows, tangled in his hair or beard!\\
---It may have been a water-rat I speared,\\
\vin But, ugh! it sounded like a baby's shriek.\\!

22.\\
Glad was I when I reached the other bank.\\
\vin Now for a better country. Vain presage!\\
\vin Who were the strugglers, what war did they wage\\
Whose savage trample thus could pad the dank\\
Soil to a plash? toads in a poisoned tank,\\
\vin Or wild cats in a redhot iron cage---\\!

23.\\
The fight must so have seemed in that fell cirque.\\
\vin What kept them there, with all the plain to choose?\\
\vin No foot-print leading to that horrid mews,\\
None out of it: mad brewage set to work\\
Their brains, no doubt, like galley-slaves the Turk\\
\vin Pits for his pastime, Christians against Jews.\\!

24.\\
And more than that---a furlong on---why, there!\\
\vin What bad use was that engine for, that wheel,\\
\vin Or brake, not wheel---that harrow fit to reel\\
Men's bodies out like silk? with all the air\\
Of Tophet's tool, on earth left unaware,\\
\vin Or brought to sharpen its rusty teeth of steel.\\!

25.\\
Then came a bit of stubbed ground, once a wood,\\
\vin Next a marsh, it would seem, and now mere earth\\
\vin Desperate and done with; (so a fool finds mirth,\\
Makes a thing and then mars it, till his mood\\
Changes and off he goes!) within a rood\\
\vin Bog, clay and rubble, sand and stark black dearth.\\!

26.\\
Now blotches rankling, coloured gay and grim,\\
\vin Now patches where some leanness of the soil's\\
\vin Broke into moss or substances like boils;\\
Then came some palsied oak, a cleft in him\\
Like a distorted mouth that splits its rim\\
\vin Gaping at death, and dies while it recoils.\\!

27.\\
And just as far as ever from the end!\\
\vin Nought in the distance but the evening, nought,\\
\vin To point my footstep further! At the thought,\\
A great black bird, Apollyon's bosom-friend,\\
Sailed past, nor beat his wide wing dragon-penned\\
\vin That brushed my cap---perchance the guide I sought.\\!

28.\\
For, looking up aware I somehow grew,\\
\vin 'Spite of the dusk, the plain had given place\\
\vin All round to mountains---with such name to grace\\
Mere ugly heights and heaps now stol'n in view.\\
How thus they had surprised me,---solve it, you!\\
\vin How to get from them was no plainer case.\\!

29.\\
Yet half I seemed to recognise some trick\\
\vin Of mischief happened to me, God knows when---\\
\vin In a bad dream perhaps. Here ended, then,\\
Progress this way. When, in the very nick\\
Of giving up, one time more, came a click\\
\vin As when a trap shuts---you're inside the den!\\!

30.\\
Burningly it came on me all at once,\\
\vin This was the place! those two hills on the right\\
\vin Crouched like two bulls locked horn in horn in fight---\\
While to the left, a tall scalped mountain . . . Dunce,\\
Fool, to be dozing at the very nonce,\\
\vin After a life spent training for the sight!\\!

31.\\
What in the midst lay but the Tower itself?\\
\vin The round squat turret, blind as the fool's heart,\\
\vin Built of brown stone, without a counterpart\\
In the whole world. The tempest's mocking elf\\
Points to the shipman thus the unseen shelf\\
\vin He strikes on, only when the timbers start.\\!

32.\\
Not see? because of night perhaps?---Why, day\\
\vin Came back again for that! before it left,\\
\vin The dying sunset kindled through a cleft:\\
The hills, like giants at a hunting, lay---\\
Chin upon hand, to see the game at bay,---\\
\vin ``Now stab and end the creature---to the heft!''\\!

33.\\
Not hear? when noise was everywhere? it tolled\\
\vin Increasing like a bell. Names in my ears,\\
\vin Of all the lost adventurers my peers,---\\
How such an one was strong, and such was bold,\\
And such was fortunate, yet each of old\\
\vin Lost, lost! one moment knelled the woe of years.\\!

34.\\
There they stood, ranged along the hill-sides---met\\
\vin To view the last of me, a living frame\\
\vin For one more picture! in a sheet of flame\\
I saw them and I knew them all. And yet\\
Dauntless the slug-horn to my lips I set\\
\vin And blew. ``Childe Roland to the Dark Tower came.''
\end{verse}
